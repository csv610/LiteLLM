**Medical Device Information: Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) Scanner**

**1. Device Name and Classification:**

* **Device Name:** Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) Scanner
* **Classification (US FDA):** Class 2 – Medical Device (Imaging Equipment) – Requires 510(k) premarket notification.
* **Classification (EU CE Marking):**  MRI scanners fall under Class I or II depending on the specific features and intended use, requiring CE marking according to Annex B of the Medical Devices Directive (MDD) or the Medical Devices Regulation (MDR).


**2. Intended Use and Applications:**

MRI scanners are intended for the detection and diagnosis of a wide range of conditions within the human body.  Specifically, they are used for:
* **Neurology:**  Brain tumors, stroke, multiple sclerosis, spinal cord disorders, epilepsy.
* **Musculoskeletal:**  Joint injuries (ligament, tendon, cartilage), bone tumors, arthritis.
* **Cardiovascular:**  Heart disease, congenital heart defects, vascular imaging.
* **Abdominal & Pelvic:** Liver disease, kidney disease, pancreatic cancer, gynecological conditions.
* **Breast Imaging:**  Detection of breast cancer and assessment of breast tissue.


**3. Technical Specifications and Principles:**

* **Principle:** MRI utilizes the magnetic properties of atomic nuclei (primarily hydrogen) within the body. A strong magnetic field aligns these nuclei. Radiofrequency (RF) pulses are then emitted, perturbing this alignment. As the nuclei return to their equilibrium state, they emit signals that are detected and processed to create detailed images.
* **Magnetic Field Strength:** Typically ranges from 1.5 Tesla (T) to 3.0 T. Higher field strengths generally provide improved signal-to-noise ratio and image quality.
* **Gradient Coils:**  Precisely controlled magnetic field gradients (X, Y, Z) are used to spatially encode the emitted and received signals, allowing for the creation of 3D images.
* **Pulse Sequences:**  A vast array of pulse sequences (e.g., T1-weighted, T2-weighted, FLAIR, diffusion-weighted imaging) are employed to highlight different tissue characteristics and pathologies.


**4. Physical Specifications:**

* **Dimensions:**  Vary significantly depending on the scanner type (open, closed, research). Typical dimensions for a clinical scanner range from 7m x 4m x 3m (length x width x height).
* **Weight:**  Approximately 8-15 tons (depending on the magnet type and size).
* **Magnet Type:**
    * **Permanent Magnet Systems:** Utilize permanent magnets (Samarium Cobalt, Neodymium) – generally smaller, lower cost, but limited field strength.
    * **Resistive Systems:** Employ coils of resistive wire to generate the magnetic field – offer higher field strengths and greater flexibility.
    * **Superconducting Magnet Systems:** Utilize superconducting coils cooled with liquid helium to achieve the highest field strengths (3.0T and above) – most common in clinical settings.
* **Patient Table:**  Adjustable in height and angle for optimal patient comfort and image acquisition.


**5. Operational Requirements and Specifications:**

* **Cooling System:** Superconducting magnets require a sophisticated cooling system (liquid helium) to maintain cryogenic temperatures.
* **Control Software:**  Sophisticated software controls the scanner’s operation, including pulse sequence selection, image acquisition parameters, and data processing.
* **RF Transmitter & Receiver:** High-power RF transmitter and sensitive RF receiver for generating and detecting signals.
* **Data Storage & Processing:**  High-capacity data storage and powerful image processing capabilities.


**6. Safety and Regulatory Information:**

* **Magnetic Field Hazards:** Strong magnetic fields pose a risk to patients with implanted metallic devices (pacemakers, defibrillators, aneurysm clips, etc.). Strict screening protocols are mandatory.
* **RF Radiation Exposure:**  Compliance with FCC and other regulatory standards regarding RF radiation exposure.
* **Emergency Shutdown Systems:**  Redundant emergency shutdown systems to quickly terminate the scan in case of malfunction.
* **Regulatory Compliance:**  FDA 510(k) clearance (US), CE Marking (EU), and adherence to relevant international standards (IEC 62304, IEC 60601).


**7. Clinical Applications and Benefits:**

* **High Soft Tissue Contrast:** MRI provides superior contrast for visualizing soft tissues compared to X-ray or CT.
* **No Ionizing Radiation:**  MRI does not use ionizing radiation, minimizing patient exposure.
* **Multiplanar Imaging:**  Ability to acquire images in multiple planes (axial, sagittal, coronal) for comprehensive assessment.
* **Functional MRI (fMRI):**  Allows for the study of brain activity by detecting changes in blood flow.



**8. Maintenance and Servicing Requirements:**

* **Routine Maintenance:**  Regular inspection of coils, cooling systems, and control software.
* **Preventative Maintenance:** Scheduled maintenance by qualified service technicians.
* **Calibration:** Periodic calibration of magnetic field strength and gradient coils.
* **Liquid Helium Management:**  Strict protocols for liquid helium handling and replenishment.


**9. Cost and Availability Information:**

* **Initial Cost:**  $1.5 million - $5 million (depending on field strength, scanner type, and features).
* **Annual Maintenance Costs:** $50,000 - $200,000 (depending on usage and service agreements).
* **Availability:**  MRI scanners are manufactured by major medical equipment companies (GE Healthcare, Siemens Healthineers, Philips Healthcare). Lead times can vary from 6-12 months.

**10. Advantages and Limitations:**

* **Advantages:**  Excellent soft tissue contrast, no ionizing radiation, multiplanar imaging, functional imaging capabilities.
* **Limitations:**  High cost, patient claustrophobia, contraindications for patients with certain metallic implants, relatively long scan times compared to CT.
